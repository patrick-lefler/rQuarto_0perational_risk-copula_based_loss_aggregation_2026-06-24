# The Diversification Question — Copula-Based Aggregation of Operational Risk Losses — Project Instructions

## 1. Project Overview
- **Short Description:** Models dependence between five operational loss categories using copulas, comparing diversified joint-loss capital against the naive sum of standalone category VaRs to quantify the model risk embedded in the diversification assumption itself.
- **Description:** This project simulates ten years of synthetic operational loss history across five Basel event-type categories for NexaCore Financial Technologies using a compound Poisson-lognormal frequency-severity framework. Rather than modeling each category's tail in isolation, the analysis fits and compares five copula families (Gaussian, Student-t, Clayton, Gumbel, Frank) to the joint loss structure, selects the best-fitting dependence model by AIC, and uses it to simulate the joint annual loss distribution. The central comparison is between the diversified 99.9% VaR computed on this joint distribution and the additive sum of standalone category VaRs — the implicit assumption under a silo-by-silo capital approach. A secondary analysis shows how much the estimated diversification benefit itself shifts depending on which copula family is assumed, framing dependence-structure choice as a source of model risk in its own right.
- **Abstract:** *[Draft below; finalize once results are in. Target 150–250 words per Deliverables Checklist.]*

  Operational risk capital is typically estimated category by category and then summed, an approach that implicitly assumes perfect dependence between loss types. This project tests that assumption directly. Using ten years of synthetic loss data across five operational risk categories — internal fraud, external fraud, ICT and business disruption, execution and process failures, and conduct risk — the analysis fits five copula families to the joint loss structure and selects the best fit by AIC. The resulting joint distribution is used to compute a diversified 99.9% annual VaR, which is then compared against the naive sum of standalone category VaRs. The gap between the two is the diversification benefit, and it is large enough to matter for capital planning. The analysis goes a step further by recomputing this benefit under each candidate copula family rather than only the AIC-selected one. The estimate moves meaningfully depending on which dependence structure is assumed, which means the choice of copula is not a modeling footnote — it is itself a material driver of the capital number, and one that deserves the same scrutiny as the severity assumptions that usually get all the attention.

- **Target audience:** Key risk stakeholders and/or board members (CRO, Operational Risk Committee)
- **Output format:** HTML
- **Render command:** `quarto render index.qmd`
- **Expected output path:** `output/index.html`
- **Data source(s):** Synthetic — generated by `scripts/generate_losses.R`. Monthly loss events simulated over a 10-year horizon (120 months) across 5 categories. Approximate record count: ~3,000–6,000 individual loss events pre-aggregation (category-dependent Poisson λ), aggregated to 10 annual joint observations per Monte Carlo path × 50,000 paths for the copula simulation stage.

- **[2026-06-23]** — **Resolved:** copula stage restricted to a bivariate fit on the one pair with genuine dependence (ICT & Business Disruption ↔ Execution, Delivery & Process Mgmt), rather than a 5-dimensional joint copula across all categories. The other three categories (Internal Fraud, External Fraud, Clients & Business Practices) are independent by construction in `generate_losses.R` and are simulated independently in `joint_simulation.R`. Rationale: a single-parameter 5-D exchangeable copula would average the real ICT-Execution signal against three pairs with no true dependence, diluting the effect the project demonstrates, and at n=10 annual observations risks fitting sampling noise as structure. Vine copula construction (full pairwise, non-exchangeable) was considered and set aside as disproportionate build complexity for a 5-category structure with only one true dependence link; `VineCopula::BiCopSelect()` is retained as a cross-check on the bivariate fit only, per §8.
- **[2026-06-23]** — `fit_copulas.R` executed successfully. Empirical Kendall's τ = 0.156, Spearman's ρ = 0.152 — modest but real dependence, consistent with the contagion mechanism firing in most months but annual aggregation compressing the year-to-year signal. AIC comparison was not decisive: t(df=2) wins (AIC=0.365) but t(df=4), t(df=6), and Gumbel all sit within ~1 AIC unit, i.e. comparable statistical support at n=10 — this directly motivates the §3.3.5 sensitivity table rather than trusting one winner. Clayton converged to a wrong-signed θ=−0.29 (poor fit, reflected in its AIC=1.71; not a bug). **Resolved:** `VineCopula::BiCopSelect()` cross-check found a Tawn type-1 extreme-value copula outperforms all five candidate families (AIC=−0.679). Decision: retain t(df=2) as the primary selection — it is the best fit within the five families the project explicitly set out to compare — and document the Tawn finding in §3.3.3 as an acknowledged scope limitation (five standard families tested, not an exhaustive search) rather than expanding the candidate set or re-running the prior stage.
- **[2026-06-24]** — `joint_simulation.R` first run flagged an internal inconsistency: the script drew an independent Monte Carlo sample for the "primary" headline report and a second independent sample for the same family (t, df=2) inside the sensitivity loop, producing two different numbers (~0.8% apart, ordinary Monte Carlo noise) for what is nominally the same scenario in two places in the eventual document. **Resolved:** rewritten so the sensitivity loop draws once per family and the primary headline report reads directly off that table's matching row — single source of truth, no duplicate draw.
- **[2026-06-24]** — **Key finding, reframes §3.3.5's intended narrative:** the diversification benefit is *not* highly sensitive to copula family choice — VaR benefit ranges 28.9%–30.0% and ES benefit 33.7%–34.0% across all five candidates, roughly a 1-point spread. Cause: Clients & Business Practices and Internal Fraud (both independent, both heavy-tailed: severity σ=1.3 and 1.2 respectively) together account for the majority of the additive VaR total; most of the diversification benefit comes from summing independent risks, not from the copula assumption on the one dependent pair. §3.3.5 will be written around this as the central insight — the capital estimate is robust to the dependence-family choice, and the reason is structural (only a fraction of the loss base carries modeled dependence at all) — rather than the originally anticipated "the estimate moves a lot depending on the copula" framing. This is a stronger, more board-relevant point and should lead the section.
- **[2026-06-24]** — Corrected `joint_simulation.R` re-run confirmed: primary headline numbers (VaR_999=17,534,295; ES_999=20,139,572) now match the t(df=2) row of `var_comparison.csv` exactly — duplicate-draw inconsistency fully resolved. Low-sensitivity finding held up and tightened slightly under the corrected single-draw-per-family version: VaR benefit 29.5%–30.7%, ES benefit 33.7%–34.1% across the five families. All three pre-computation scripts (`generate_losses.R`, `fit_copulas.R`, `joint_simulation.R`) confirmed running cleanly. Final headline figures for the document: additive VaR_999=$24,870,683 vs. diversified VaR_999=$17,534,295 (29.5% benefit); additive ES_999=$30,403,652 vs. diversified ES_999=$20,139,572 (33.8% benefit).
